## 4. FIRST LINKEDIN ARTICLE

**Title:** The Most Overlooked Revenue Line in Independent Hotels

---

Every independent hotel GM I've ever met knows their ADR. They know their occupancy. Most know their RevPAR within a dollar or two.

Almost none of them know how much money their shuttle is leaving on the table.

Here's a number that changed how I think about hotel operations:

**$7,820.**

That's what one Fort Lauderdale hotel — 121 rooms, independent, competing directly with chains near Port Everglades — earned from shuttle and transport bookings in four months.

Not from food and beverage. Not from room upgrades. From the shuttle they were already running.

Here's what happened, and why most properties will never see that number.

---

### The Problem With How Hotels Capture Transport Revenue

Most hotels with a shuttle have some version of the same system:

A guest calls the front desk. The front desk writes down the request. Someone tells the driver. Sometimes the driver shows up. Sometimes there's a miscommunication. Sometimes the guest calls an Uber instead.

That last part — the guest calling an Uber instead — is where the revenue disappears.

At a property near a cruise port or major airport, shuttle runs are one of the highest-margin ancillary services a hotel can offer. The vehicle already exists. The driver is already on payroll. The marginal cost of an additional run is near zero.

But capturing that booking requires friction-free access at the moment the guest decides they need a ride.

That moment is almost never "when the guest calls the front desk."

It's when they're in their room the night before checkout, opening their laptop to book a car.

---

### What Changed at This Property

The Fort Lauderdale hotel added one thing: a QR code in every room that let guests request transport directly, without calling the front desk.

Guests could select:
→ Hotel shuttle (free or fee-based)
→ Third-party taxi (hotel-arranged)

They entered their pickup time. The front desk got notified instantly. The driver was assigned. The guest got a confirmation.

No phone call. No lost sticky note. No miscommunication.

In the first four months:
- 94 shuttle bookings captured through the system
- Average booking value: $83
- Zero no-shows (guests who booked confirmed; guests who didn't were already gone)
- Front desk fielded 60% fewer transport-related phone calls

The revenue wasn't new. It was already there. They just finally had a system to capture it.

---

### The Broader Pattern

Shuttle revenue is one category. But this pattern repeats across every ancillary service an independent hotel offers.

Late checkout. In-room dining. Activity bookings. Airport transfers.

Every one of these has a "moment of decision" that happens when the guest is alone in their room, not when they're standing at the front desk.

The hotels that capture that revenue are the ones that meet guests at that moment — with a frictionless, no-download-required way to ask for what they need.

The ones that don't are the ones watching their RevPAR stay flat while their guests spend money somewhere else.

---

### What To Do About It

If you're running an independent hotel with a shuttle, here's where to start:

**1. Count your shuttle runs for the last 90 days.**
How many were captured as formal bookings vs. informal requests that may or may not have happened?

**2. Calculate what you're actually charging.**
Flat fee? Per-person? Free? If it's free, is there a reason it has to be?

**3. Look at what guests are spending on Uber or Lyft in your market.**
If a Lyft to the airport is $35 and your shuttle costs nothing, you're subsidizing a service that guests would happily pay for.

**4. Remove the friction.**
If booking the shuttle requires a phone call, you're losing bookings. Put the option in front of guests when they're making the decision — in their room, the night before.

---

The $7,820 wasn't exceptional performance. It was a baseline that most 100+ room properties near a major transit hub should be able to hit.

The only thing that was exceptional was that this hotel had a system to capture it.
